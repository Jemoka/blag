For two non-zero integers, greatest common divisor denoted \(\gcd (a,b)\) is the largest \(d\) such that \(d|a\) and \(d|b\)

additional information

\(1\) and \(0\)

\begin{equation} a | 0, \forall a \in \mathbb{Z} \end{equation}

and

\begin{equation} 1 | a, \forall a \in \mathbb{Z} \end{equation}

properties of the gcd

For two integers not both \(0\) named \(a,b\). \(\gcd (a,b) = as+bt\) for some \(s\) and \(t\). In fact, the gcd is the smallest integer (“integral”) linear combination of \(a\) and \(b\).

coprime

When \(\gcd (a,b)=1\), they are called coprime.

And therefore, because of the properties of the gcd, when \(a\) and \(b\) are coprime, there is some \(s,t\) such that \(as+bt = 1\).
